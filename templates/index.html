What are PFAS?

PFAS (Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances) are a large family of more than 4,700 man-made chemicals that have been used since the 1950s in industry and consumer goods. They are extremely resistant to water, grease, and heat. These properties make PFAS valuable in everyday products, but also very dangerous to the environment and human health.

PFAS are often called ‚Äúforever chemicals‚Äù because they hardly break down in nature. Once released into the environment, they can contaminate drinking water, accumulate in fish and livestock, and remain in the human body for years.

Studies suggest links between PFAS exposure and health issues such as liver problems, thyroid disease, developmental delays, and even cancer.

PFAS in the Netherlands

In the Netherlands, PFAS contamination has been found in several rivers, lakes, and soil sites. These chemicals can spread from industrial facilities into the wider environment.

PFAS near the Amstel in Amsterdam

Recent environmental monitoring has shown traces of PFAS in surface waters such as the Amstel river. While the levels are being closely studied, it raises concern because the Amstel is not only an important waterway for the city but also connects to broader ecosystems. Authorities continue to monitor and research safe levels of PFAS in this area.

Where are PFAS found?

  • Non-stick cookware (e.g., Teflon pans)
  • Waterproof clothing and outdoor gear
  • Food packaging such as fast-food wrappers and pizza boxes
  • Firefighting foams used at airports and military bases
  • Industrial coatings, paints, and cleaning products

Why are PFAS a problem?

Because PFAS do not naturally degrade, they accumulate over time in water, soil, and living organisms. This means that every year, the levels of PFAS in our environment can grow unless strict regulations and clean-up actions are taken.

Health Risks

Research has linked PFAS exposure to:

  • Increased cholesterol levels
  • Liver and kidney damage
  • Weakened immune system (less effective vaccines)
  • Thyroid disease
  • Pregnancy complications and low birth weight
  • Developmental issues in children
  • Higher risk of certain cancers (kidney, testicular)

What can you do?

  • Choose PFAS-free cookware such as stainless steel or cast iron
  • Avoid water- and stain-repellent products unless PFAS-free
  • Check if your drinking water supplier monitors PFAS levels
  • Support environmental initiatives that regulate and clean PFAS
